No. 14 · Policy & People

The Compression Problem

How managing information up and down a hierarchy is the defining challenge for people and for AI alike.

Abstract. Government and large enterprises face a paradox when modernizing legacy systems at scale. The transformation of the Government of Alberta's fourteen hundred systems, representing four hundred sixty-six million lines of code across four thousand repositories, requires compressing forty years of accumulated technical debt, institutional knowledge, and embedded business logic into a four-year window, at a target of twentyfold faster delivery and about a ninety-five percent reduction in cost. This white paper argues that the core challenge is informational rather than technical: how to maintain fidelity of ground truth as information moves up and down organizational hierarchies, where each layer compresses what it communicates upward and decompresses what it receives downward. This compression problem is not unique to artificial intelligence; it is the reason hierarchical organizations exist. AI introduces both a solution and a new set of risks. By enabling information fidelity across parallel agents, instant knowledge propagation, and operation at machine speed, AI can theoretically flatten organizational structures and maintain coherence across unprecedented scale. Yet this only works if we solve the compression problem: ensuring that strategic intent survives countless handoffs, that ground truth stays synchronized across thousands of concurrent agents, and that humans remain meaningfully in the loop despite operating in a system orders of magnitude faster than human cognition.
When we speak of transforming government, we are not starting from a blank slate. Government operates according to legislation, regulations, and policies that form one layer of ground truth. But there exists a second, often invisible layer of ground truth: the accumulated processes, business rules, and institutional logic embedded in forty years of legacy code and tribal knowledge. The Government of Alberta is attempting to compress that estate, roughly 466 million lines of code across 1,400 systems and 4,000 repositories, into a four-year rebuild, increasing the speed of delivery twentyfold and reducing its cost by about ninety-five percent. The hardest part of that work is informational rather than technical, and this paper calls it the compression problem.


## §01 Where the truth of government lives

This embedded ground truth exists in multiple forms. Some of it is passive, knowledge that persists in people's heads, undocumented and fragile. But much of it has been actively codified. Workflows, data structures, permissions matrices, business logic, and decision trees have been baked into applications over decades. These are not arbitrary constructs. They represent the evolved wisdom of how government actually works, refined through countless edge cases, policy changes, and operational challenges. When we seek to modernize a system, we face a critical choice: do we extract and preserve this embedded ground truth, or do we discard it and rebuild from policy documents alone? The answer matters because policy documents often describe how government should work, while code describes how government actually works, and these are frequently misaligned. The first dimension of the compression problem, then, is this: code itself is a form of documentation. It is an archive of institutional process, constraint, and learning. To compress forty years of technical debt without losing institutional memory, we must first excavate what is actually in the code, and distinguish between what should be preserved, what should be reimagined, and what should be discarded.

But extracting ground truth from code is only the beginning. Even if we successfully identify what government systems actually do, we face a second problem: abstraction inevitably loses information. Consider a geographic information system used across multiple government ministries. At the surface level, we might call this a "GIS module", a reasonable abstraction that captures the idea that geospatial data matters to public safety, forestry, environmental management, and land administration. But beneath that abstraction lie hundreds or thousands of functions: different layers of data, specialized formulas, metadata interactions, permissions rules, and dependencies that are invisible when we collapse everything into a single term. When a business stakeholder hears "GIS module", they may nod in agreement in principle. They understand that geospatial management is important. But this agreement glosses over critical specificity. It implements what we might call a lossy compression algorithm, one where detail is stripped away in service of simplicity, with the assumption that the compressed version is good enough. Except it often is not good enough. The client later discovers that certain functions are missing, or that the system does not handle a particular edge case that was critical in the legacy system. This is the second dimension of the compression problem: at every level of organizational communication, we compress complex systems into simpler terms. We do this necessarily, because human cognition cannot hold the full complexity of large systems in mind at once. But this compression always carries a cost. Information is lost. Nuance disappears. And the person receiving the compressed message may believe they understand when, in fact, critical details have been abstracted away.

The scale of the estate 466M lines. Roughly 466 million lines of code across 1,400 systems and 4,000 repositories, forty years of accumulated technical debt to be rebuilt in four.


## §02 The hierarchy as a compression machine

The pyramid-shaped organizational hierarchy that dominates government and enterprise is a practical solution to an unsolvable problem, not an accident of history or an artifact of outdated management thinking. The problem is this: how do you get the information from a thousand people to one person in a single day? Consider the mathematics. If a leader has a thousand direct reports, and each report takes one hour to absorb, the leader would need a thousand hours in a day, an obvious impossibility. The organizational hierarchy solves this through compression. Information flows upward through layers of management, with each layer applying judgment to decide what is signal and what is noise. Middle managers act as compression gates, filtering out low-relevance information and passing only what is most important to the next level up. We might visualize this as a sigmoidal compression curve. As information passes upward, roughly half of it is deemed less relevant and is abstracted or lost, and the remaining half is passed up. By the time information reaches the top of the organization, it has undergone perhaps a thousand-fold lossy compression. Only the most critical signal survives.

This solves the time problem. But it creates a different problem: fidelity loss at every layer. A frontline worker sees the full complexity of a situation. Their manager sees a simplified version. The director sees a further simplified version. By the time insight reaches the executive, it has been compressed so many times that critical nuance may have disappeared entirely. Yet the hierarchy is also a decompression machine. When a leader sets strategic direction, say, ten broad objectives, those directives must be expanded as they move downward. A CEO's statement that "we will be highly human-centered" does not translate directly into action. Middle managers must decompress that vision, apply their judgment about what it means in their specific context, and translate it into tactical actions that frontline workers can execute. Here too, fidelity is at risk. The expansion creates voids. As strategic direction becomes broader and more abstract, gaps open up, and these gaps must be filled by someone. When humans fill them through judgment, we call it leadership, either good leadership if the judgment aligns with intent, or bad management if it misaligns. The hierarchy persists because it is the only practical way humans have found to compress information going up and decompress strategy going down at scale.

"The purpose of a hierarchy is an information compression and decompression system. Give the leader a thousand times the time and capacity, and you would not need one at all." · Janak Alford, Deputy Minister, Ministry of Technology and Innovation


## §03 Decompression is the hallucination problem

There is a direct equivalence between what we call "hallucination" in artificial intelligence and what we call "judgment" or "leadership failure" in human management. When an AI system is asked to expand a five-line business requirement into a fifty-page business plan, a process the Academy calls hydration, gaps inevitably emerge. The AI must infer details that were not specified. When it fills those gaps with inferences that do not align with the original intent, we call this a hallucination. It is a failure of decompression, a failure to faithfully expand compressed information. But humans do exactly the same thing. When a manager receives a strategic directive and must translate it into specific actions for their team, they too are decompressing. They are inferring details. They are filling gaps. When they do this well and the outcome aligns with intent, we celebrate their leadership. When they do it poorly, we call it failed management. Structurally, it is the same process with the same failure mode. The difference lies in our judgment of the outcome, not in the process. We have different vocabulary for when humans get it wrong versus when AI gets it wrong. But the underlying problem is identical: compression always creates information loss, and decompression always requires inference to fill the gaps created by that loss.


## §04 How AI changes the constraints

Artificial intelligence disrupts the assumptions that made hierarchies necessary. First, AI can hold vastly more information in active context than humans. Current large language models can maintain a million tokens of context, and future models will hold more. It is entirely plausible to have a single AI system that concurrently holds and processes status updates, decision logs, and contextual information from five hundred or five thousand employees at once, performing inference and synthesis across all of them. Second, AI can be instantiated in parallel. Instead of a single CEO who can only be in one place at one time, you could have a thousand instances of a CEO-level AI, each with identical strategic vision and reasoning capacity, each able to engage with different streams of issues concurrently. Each employee could be paired with an AI coach that understands both the strategic vision and the ground truth of their specific situation.

Third, and most importantly, AI can share information with perfect fidelity. When one robotic surgeon learns a new technique, every robotic surgeon instantly has that technique. When one AI agent discovers an optimal solution to a problem, that solution is instantly available to all parallel instances. There is no game-of-telephone effect, and no degradation through layers of human interpretation. Knowledge propagates losslessly across the entire system. This means you could theoretically eliminate many of the middle management layers that exist solely to compress and decompress information. You could invert the pyramid entirely, having ten auditors, security reviewers, and quality officers examining every decision, all providing feedback with perfect coherence because they share unified memory and reasoning. But this only works if you solve the compression problem in this new context. The constraints have changed, while the challenge remains: how do you maintain fidelity of ground truth and strategic intent as information flows through thousands of agents, each with limited context windows, each requiring handoffs when its context fills and must be purged?


## §05 Speed changes the equation

There is one more variable that changes the equation: speed. In traditional government hierarchies, an information request typically takes a month to traverse down through layers of management, be answered at the ground level, and travel back up through layers of review and vetting. By the time a deputy minister receives an answer to their question, weeks have passed, and the information has been compressed and recompressed at each layer, its fidelity degrading accordingly. Now imagine that same request-and-response cycle completes in one second. Strategic decisions could be made, implemented, and evaluated in near-real time, and the mathematics of the hierarchy problem changes radically. If a leader has a thousand employees and each has an issue that takes one hour to address, the leader cannot possibly handle all of them; the math is impossible. But if each issue is identified, routed, addressed, and resolved within one second, the math becomes different. A leader, or more realistically a strategically aligned AI, could theoretically field tens of thousands of issues daily, because the time bottleneck that created the need for compression layers no longer exists.

Speed does not eliminate the compression problem. It transforms it from a necessity into a design choice. When latency is measured in seconds or milliseconds rather than days or weeks, you have the option to flatten organizational structures radically. You could have a thousand concurrent strategic leaders, an inverted pyramid of oversight, or hybrid models that would have been impossible in a slower system. Speed also changes institutional constraints. Government processes like annual budget cycles, coming-into-force periods, and consultation windows exist partly because they are the speed at which human bureaucracy can move. They are not ground truth. They are artifacts of organizational constraint, and if those constraints dissolve, so might the institutions built around them. No government values a slow IT function; if a policy could be implemented faithfully across its systems the next day, government would prefer it.


## §06 The human limit, and richer ways to communicate

Yet there remains a constraint that speed and AI capability cannot bypass: human comprehension. A deputy minister cannot absorb four hundred sixty-six million lines of code. A premier cannot read a thousand pages of technical architecture and make an informed decision. Even if you could compress all of government's digital systems into a perfect, lossless AI representation, presenting that to a human decision-maker would still require another round of compression, because human language operates at roughly forty bits per second, while AI operates orders of magnitude faster. This is the final dimension of the compression problem: humans and AI operate at different speeds and with different information fidelity. The traditional solution has been the briefing note, a two-month effort to compress months of analysis into a few pages. But this is itself an artifact of the compression problem. It assumes that humans can only absorb information in linguistic form at linguistic speed.

Human bandwidth ~40 bits/s. The rough rate at which language carries meaning between people, orders of magnitude slower than the machine, which is why a perfect AI model of government still has to be compressed to reach a human decision-maker.

Yet humans have other cognitive capacities that operate far faster than language. The visual cortex processes information orders of magnitude faster than auditory processing, and spatial reasoning, pattern recognition, and interactive exploration tap into cognitive systems that bypass the bottleneck of linguistic comprehension. For humans to remain meaningfully in the loop in an AI-augmented transformation, we may need to shift how we communicate with them. Rather than dense briefing notes, decision-makers might need animated visualizations, interactive simulations, video explorations, or even gamified environments that let them explore complex systems through multiple sensory channels at once. The point is to make information richer rather than simpler, to present it in forms that allow human cognitive systems to absorb fidelity at speeds closer to what AI operates at. This is also why this work is sixteen white papers, providing hours of reading and listening. A single page cannot contain sufficient fidelity, but a comprehensive evidence base can be vetted by independent validators, including AI systems, and then compressed into a briefing that gives a decision-maker genuine confidence. The decision-maker does not need to understand every detail. They need to understand that the analysis has been rigorous, that the ground truth has been carefully excavated, that independent parties have vetted the logic, and that the proposed path forward is grounded in reality rather than speculation.


## §07 The architecture of fidelity

If we accept that AI can maintain information fidelity and operate at scale through parallel agents, the practical challenge becomes how to structure the system so that fidelity actually survives. The answer lies in rigorous documentation of intent, decision logs, and architectural specifications, what we might call the knowledge infrastructure. When an agent's context window fills and it must be replaced by a successor, that successor needs to inherit the current state of the system together with a complete record of why decisions were made, what constraints exist, and what dependencies matter. Even a single application overflows and purges the million-token window many times during its build, and across 1,400 systems this happens countless times. This is analogous to how medieval monks maintained the fidelity of texts. They did not simply copy manuscripts; they created elaborate infrastructure, commentary, cross-references, illuminated margins, that helped subsequent copyists understand both what was being copied and why it mattered. The fidelity of knowledge across time required more than accurate transcription. It required context. The same is true of passing a master's skill to an apprentice with enough fidelity that the work continues unchanged when the master stops.

No such plan is ever complete. By Godel's incompleteness theorem, any system is either inconsistent or incomplete, so a strategic plan of this scale will necessarily have gaps, and a good-enough plan now is better than a perfect plan never, as long as you can measure the outcome and iterate. When the cost of an attempt is a week and a hundred thousand dollars rather than a decade and a hundred million, there is little to stop you from treating a deficient result as a reinforcement-learning opportunity and refining it. The system should behave less like a structure of precision and permanence and more like a living organism. Through cellular autophagy, worn cells are constantly destroyed and regenerated; in a modular Government 3.0 system, when a regulation changes, the affected module can be destroyed and recreated and reintroduced, with a memory of what was true and when. Resilience comes from diversity rather than monoculture, some parts deterministic and some probabilistic, some human and some not, so the whole can adapt or recover quickly. The danger this guards against is the butterfly effect: a deprecated library or a fresh cyber vulnerability, left unmanaged at the coal face, ripples into another project, a slipped deadline, a lost client. For want of a nail the kingdom was lost; the architecture of fidelity exists so that small failures are caught and absorbed before they metastasize.


## §08 Resolving ambiguity and absorbing change

What happens when ground truth is ambiguous, when regulations conflict, when reasonable people disagree about how a policy should be implemented? Some decisions must remain decisions made under explicit constraints, but these decision points should be rare and clearly marked, not the default mode of operation, and the decider need not be human as long as the evaluation framework is sound. In most cases, when multiple interpretations exist, you can run them in parallel. This is A/B testing at scale: you implement different interpretations of an ambiguous requirement in different parts of the system, measure the outcomes, and evaluate which approach performs better against your measurement framework. For genuinely irreducible conflicts, you establish a hierarchy of concerns, a weighted function that determines which value takes precedence when they compete, since real disagreements rarely sit in perfect equilibrium, like a balloon that rests on the ceiling or the floor but not in the middle of the air. And for reversible decisions, in Amazon's terms a two-way door, you can simply pursue both options at low cost, evaluate them over time, and dispose of the one that proves less valuable, since most decisions in a digital system are two-way doors. The same logic absorbs change. Ground the agents in the real laws and physics of the province as their reference, and when the world shifts, regenerate against it.


## §09 The compression problem as organizational design

The compression problem is the fundamental obstacle, and it exists at every level. It is embedded in how information flows upward through hierarchies, where each layer strips away detail. It exists in how strategy flows downward, where each layer must infer and fill gaps. It exists in the abstractions we use to communicate complex systems, in the gap between what humans can comprehend and what needs to be understood, and in the limit of any single agent's context window. For centuries, government and enterprise have solved the compression problem through hierarchy, which exists precisely because the problem is otherwise unsolvable, accepting fidelity loss as the price of scaling human attention. Artificial intelligence offers a different solution. By enabling information sharing across parallel agents, operating at machine speed, and maintaining unified memory across thousands of concurrent instances, AI can flatten hierarchies and maintain coherence at scales that would be impossible for humans alone. AI does not eliminate the compression problem. It transforms it. The new challenge is maintaining fidelity across countless context-window boundaries, ensuring that strategic intent survives thousands of handoffs, and keeping humans meaningfully in the loop despite a system orders of magnitude faster than human cognition.

There are real limits, and naming them is part of the argument. The hard physical one is compute: at full scale the inference required may exceed what is available, and the true cost has not yet been calculated. There is a relative-speed problem too: compressing forty years of debt into four matters less if the world outside accelerates faster still, the way a faster car stops helping once everyone else takes to the air. And the binding constraint, in the end, is human comprehension. Even if systems could be rebuilt hourly, our ability to understand the change would govern how fast it is wise to move. The model holds as long as it stays synchronized to the ground truth of reality, its laws, its data, and its present state, and drifts into failure only when it loses that synchrony.

The target, and the consolidation 20× / 95%. A twentyfold increase in delivery speed and about a ninety-five percent reduction in cost, alongside a consolidation of roughly 200 ministry systems into 12 to 16 modular components without losing the fidelity of their function.


## §10 This paper as evidence of the compression problem

This white paper itself is an artifact of the compression problem. What you are reading represents hours of conversation, exploration, refinement, and back-and-forth thinking, compressed into a coherent narrative. Ideas that emerged messily have been organized, tangents have been integrated or pruned, and clarifications have been woven into the main thread, with the voice edited for clarity while preserving authenticity. But this compression comes at a cost. Nuance has been lost. The exploratory thinking that led to the insights has been abstracted away, and the moments of uncertainty and correction have been polished into a smooth argument. To make this transparent, the complete unedited transcript of the conversation that produced this paper is preserved in full in the accompanying repository and referenced from the About section. Anyone reading this white paper can compare the polished final version against the raw transcript, see where compression occurred, what was preserved and what was abstracted, and how messy thinking becomes a coherent argument. That gap is evidence of the argument itself. The compression problem is demonstrated in real time in the gap between the transcript and this white paper. If the compression from transcript to paper kept faith with the intent, that is some evidence that the larger compression, from 466 million lines of code to a decision a premier can make, can be done with fidelity too.

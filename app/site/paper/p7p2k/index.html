No. 06 · Technical

The Well-Built Harness

How AI lets the public service build and ship enterprise-grade software at speed, without lowering the bar.

Abstract. Early in Alberta's AI Maximalist program, coding tools like Cursor, Lovable, and Claude Code collapsed the time to build a working prototype from roughly six weeks to thirty minutes. The speed was genuine, but the prototypes could not be reproduced, they skipped the controls a public service requires, and the agents reported themselves finished when they were not. This paper describes the working environment Technology and Innovation built to close that gap, the harness, a combination of skills, standards, and pre-built templates that lets a coding agent take on the technical work while people keep the vision, the architecture, and the decisions that matter. The harness, the templates, and the skill files are released as open source.
Large language models (LLM) have a strong capacity to develop application code. Early in our AI Maximalist program in 2025, Technology and Innovation staff began to evaluate these tools to see how they could be deployed within our government environment. A range of tools were explored, such as Cursor, Lovable, and Claude Code, which was identified as the most effective and popular at the time. These tools made application development fast, easy for even non-technical people, and the time to transform an idea into a functional prototype dropped from roughly 6 weeks (as previously benchmarked by management in that unit) to as little as 30 minutes. The speed increases were so significant that Ministry subject matter experts could be present in the room as these ideas were worked out in real time, providing feedback and sign-off to allow technical teams to continue their work. This factor alone resulted in a significant increase in the speed of delivery, as the traditional back and forth between technical and non-technical staff was reduced to a single session.

In a previous whitepaper, Alberta presented the clear need to address technical debt and to increase the speed and throughput of our delivery. Technology and Innovation has a critical need to address both problems with speed. Our quality standards cannot be lowered, especially in cyber security, accessibility, and look and feel. Protecting the privacy of Albertans and maintaining the integrity of our systems is non-negotiable. AI provides a pathway to moving faster, but speed cannot be a tradeoff for quality. Nor does it have to. With the right formula, governments (and other large organizations where standards are paramount) can move quickly and accurately with AI undertaking as much as 99% of the technical work while humans drive the vision, the architecture, and make the important decisions on usability, functionality, and benefits realization.


## §01 The problem worth solving

Despite the gains in performance, several significant challenges in AI coding practices were observed by Alberta's AI Maximalists.

First, given the nature of AI, it was difficult to recreate the same application twice. Each interaction with AI resulted in a different look and feel, layout, and functionality. All were valid, however the inability to replicate the same application twice was a significant barrier to deploying AI in a government environment.

Second, these tools frequently failed to implement potentially critical security protocols. Threat actors are sophisticated, and while prototypes could be achieved quickly, it would take significant time and effort to fix the gaps in the security (even with AI assistance). And like the user interfaces, the fixes applied were also inconsistent and difficult to replicate between any two applications.

The third significant challenge was the over-confidence of the coding agent. AI agents would claim completeness in their code, but when probed would demonstrate significant gaps. AI agents are unreliable witnesses to the state and performance of their code creations, and as a result, non-technical staff were unable to accurately evaluate application readiness.

Alberta has worked with nearly every leading AI tool and large language model. We've tested what works, and more important, what doesn't. From March 2025 to May 2026, Technology and Innovation have produced far more than 1,000 applications. The vast majority are prototypes, but dozens have made it into production, offering real value. The prototypes themselves follow a pattern of exploration, experimentation, and even play. Without a sense of play, it is impossible for staff to learn the boundaries of these models, and to learn how to work effectively. It may be odd to say 'play', especially in a government environment, but that is how we humans learn. We learn best when we are in a psychologically safe environment, when we are challenged to push our boundaries, and when we are having fun. In those 1,000+ applications we've built, we've explored the limits of AI, and found its real gaps. Each insight allows us to capture and refine our own processes. We see where AI breaks down, and we find innovative ways to leverage its immense capability while also recognizing its real, and often glaring, shortcomings. Over this period of exploration and play, we've created a recipe book on how to work well.

Much of our attention has been fixated around the Anthropic Claude models. First, the models are impressive. Second, Anthropic has released tools and user interfaces which maximize their creativity, such as Claude Cowork and Claude Code, two powerful productivity suites designed for creatives and software developers respectively. Partially, we've been 'getting to know the new neighbours', in the sense that these models really seem to elicit their own idiosyncratic behaviours, if not their own personalities. These 'features' are not written anywhere, and while Anthropic has done a meaningful job at documenting some of the most notable traits, it is up to the individual to explore how these systems respond. Significantly, their variability is extremely sensitive to the user's prompting styles. Just like two people can have a very different conversation with the same person, two people can have a very different experience with the same model. How you explain yourself, introduce an idea, and even the words you use, drive extremely different outcomes. And in an environment where repeatability and quality are paramount, understanding these nuances may be the difference between an app which ships and an app which fails to achieve its outcomes.


## §02 Driving outcomes

To drive outcomes, we need to follow a set of patterns and styles that increase the probability that the model will perform the outcomes we are seeking. For a large language model, it should be no surprise that we codify that through words. Instructions, which are called prompts, explain our intent and outcome with an extremely high degree of specificity. When we want the AI to be creative, we provide outcomes-based prompts. When we want to be prescriptive, we provide instructions-based prompts. Through iteration, Alberta has assembled hundreds of these prompts into a collection called AI 'skills', instructions which literally describe the methods and intended outcomes we are achieving. These skills are highly 'opinionated', a technical term meaning that we are leaving little to the imagination of the individual, whether human or AI coder. They also are as descriptive as possible without being verbose prose, as the models themselves have limits on how much they can process. Finding that middle ground is truly an art mastered through practice.

Through rounds of trial and error, Alberta staff began to test methods of increasing the assurance in the quality and consistency of the code output. Starting with small application features (like a landing page or API), the team developed a range of standard methods, instructions for the LLM to follow in its prompt, which would narrow the creativity of the LLM by limiting uncertainty. These standards, now known as 'skill files', are provided to the coding agent at the beginning of a task. A well-written specification reduced uncertainty and resulted in a consistent output. Concurrently, vendors such as Anthropic and OpenAI released enhanced coding interfaces, such as Claude Code, which were bundled with their own instructions. The combination of the two, the vendor's instructions and Alberta's skill files, resulted in a significant increase in the quality and consistency of the code output. The systematic itemization of these steps resulted in agents which could produce consistent results without negatively impacting speed.

These instructions run inside a harness: the software scaffolding around a language model that turns the model into an agent. It holds the model's context across a session, exposes the file system and the tools, runs the loop between the model's intent and the work the agent carries out, and enforces the guardrails that keep the model on task. The agent platform supplies that scaffolding. This paper takes it as given and covers what gets assembled around it to produce software the Government of Alberta would ship.

Alberta has built that surrounding work as a small number of opinionated, open-source assemblies. Each assembly bundles four pieces. A standards file the agent reads at the start of every session, named CLAUDE.md. A set of skills, folders the agent pulls expertise from on demand. A set of hooks, commands the harness fires on its own lifecycle events. And the template the project starts from, which carries the other three already configured. The sections below take each piece in turn.


## §03 The contract and the skills

The first piece is the standards file, named CLAUDE.md, at the root of the project. The harness loads it at the start of every session and treats it as the authoritative description of what this project is. Think of the work order pinned up at the start of a job on a site. It names the project, says what good looks like, says what the agent may change, and says what the agent must never touch. New developers read it for the same reason: it tells them which trade-offs the team already settled, so they can spend their attention on the ones still open.

What lives in a CLAUDE.md One page. What the project is for. Who it serves. The standards the project holds itself to. The folder map. The commands a contributor will run. The list of things the project will not do without an explicit decision. In doubt, the CLAUDE.md is what you read.

The contract has to be short, because the agent reads it every time. It has to be precise, because the agent takes it literally. And it has to be honest about what it does not cover, because the agent fills any gap with whatever it was trained on. A good CLAUDE.md closes the gaps that matter for this project and trusts the agent's general training for the rest.

The second piece is the collection of skills. Teams call them playbooks, and this paper uses the two words interchangeably. A skill is a folder. It carries a SKILL.md file with a short name and description in its header, the body of the instructions below, and any supporting files the instructions reference. Project-level skills live at .claude/skills/ and travel with the team through version control. User-level skills live at ~/.claude/skills/ and follow an individual developer.

The design principle is progressive disclosure. At the start of a session the harness reads only the name and description of each installed skill, fifty to a hundred words apiece, enough for the agent to know when each one is relevant. The full body loads only when the agent decides the task matches. Supporting files inside the folder load only when the body references them. The reading cost paid at each step matches the depth of work being done at that step, because an agent's reading capacity is finite, the same way any reader's is.

On why a playbook is a folder, not a paragraph. A playbook carries an instruction file, a methodology, an output template, and sometimes a few helper scripts. Putting the parts together as a folder means a contributor can read the full playbook the same way the agent does, edit it in one place, and version it as a single unit when the practice changes.


## §04 Inspections that run every time

The third piece is the inspections, commonly called hooks. A hook is a command, an HTTP call, or a small subagent that fires at a moment in the agent's lifecycle that matters. It reads the work, answers a yes-or-no question, and returns an exit code the harness reads as the answer. If the answer is no, the harness blocks the action that was about to happen, surfaces the failure back to the agent, and gives the agent a chance to fix it.

The harness fires hooks on a fixed set of lifecycle events. PreToolUse fires before the agent invokes a tool, and a hook here can block the tool entirely. PostToolUse fires after a tool completes, so a hook can verify the result, log it, or push context back into the agent's view. Stop fires when the agent is about to declare the turn finished, and a hook here can make the agent keep working. SessionStart, UserPromptSubmit, PreCompact, and roughly two dozen others cover the rest of the moments a project might want to check.

The inspections are deterministic, and that is what gives the harness its teeth. They run every time, ask the same question every time, and cannot be talked out of a finding, because they were never asked for an opinion. They were asked whether the work crosses the bar that was set. An agent in a hurry, or a contributor under a deadline, cannot reason a hook out of a no. The work either crosses the bar or it does not.


## §05 Building harnesses

Building harnesses is difficult and takes extreme diligence. You need to be extremely diligent because you're not just building an app, you're curating the skills that a human might gain intuitively over 10-20 years.

You cannot vibe code or make simple prompts. Using AI to create harnesses has the 'photocopier' problem, in that each derivative version degrades the quality, causing future and unpredictable breakdowns. A harness which is 90% accurate produces another app which is 80% accurate. You need to take the time and meditate on every feature, and test (and test again) each iteration to ensure that the system is faithfully building what you need. Slop must be avoided at all costs.

You must show the AI what good looks like. In the technology space, this is by providing as near a perfect application as humanly possible as a template for it to emulate. This is similar to how arts academies throughout history learned to paint, by studying the great masters of the Renaissance, and copying and emulating until they achieve as close to perfection as possible. These 'masterpiece' templates mean everything to an AI.

"Riffing off Arthur C. Clarke's Third Law, which states any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic, I would say that any sufficiently advanced template is indistinguishable from code. The best harness is code." · Janak Alford, Deputy Minister, Ministry of Technology and Innovation

The real breakthrough came when skills were augmented with templates. Templates are pre-built codebases that have the security, accessibility, and look-and-feel standards already built in. By starting with a template, the coding agent only had to fill in the domain-specific content, which is the part it does reliably and quickly. This approach significantly reduced the time to production, as the agent was not required to re-invent the wheel on every project. Further, even the most rudimentary app could pass a robust security audit, and all applications worked 'out of the box' with the same look and feel. Paired with clear instructions on adhering to these standards (to prevent the AI from 'drifting' away from the template), the result was a significant increase in the speed of delivery while maintaining the quality and consistency required for public-sector applications.

The combination of skills, standards, templates, and clearly structured requirements allowed the AI to undertake coding tasks. The output was a method which outperformed human speed, quality, and consistency, and allowed for the speed of 'vibe coding' while enforcing a specifications-driven approach. This method is now called 'spec driven development' or 'design engineering'. Alberta bundled several unique facets into these harnesses. We created 'red team' and 'blue team' skills and scripts (deterministic, repeatable, and rules based instructions) to adversarially and cooperatively review and refine application security. We created 'green team' skills to check for code completion and check online for known vulnerabilities. We created 'yellow team' skills to detect and cleanse out the specific idiosyncratic appearance of AI-generated text content in instructions and interfaces, known now as 'AI smell'. We also added into this harness a range of specific skills for the entire application development lifecycle, from requirements structuring through testing and deployment, as well as Alberta's clear design system instructions for look and feel and accessibility.

The result was a harness which could be used by technical staff to create applications at unprecedented speed and quality. Even non-technical users found these harnesses effective in producing results which exceeded the quality of tools like Lovable, since the generated solutions were aligned to Alberta's standards. Additional harnesses are now being created daily by the team for writing, training, presentation development, data analysis, and more. Any creative or analytical process can be enhanced with a well-built harness, and the team is actively exploring new use cases and sharing them across the department. Where the Academy started with sharable simple prompts, harnesses now enable complex tasks to be automated with complete repeatability and consistency. Individuals can bundle their knowledge and skills into a harness and an entire team's collective skill and capacity increases as a result. The harness becomes a new form of sharable skill, implemented by the AI, uplifting the entire team to a new level of performance.

Alberta ships three of these template harnesses today, each tuned to a different kind of project. The common harness, in the repository AIDE-COMMON-HARNESS, is a Vue and Express starter for any public web application that does not need the Government of Alberta look and feel. The velocity harness, in AIDE-VELOCITY-HARNESS, wraps the same backend in the Government of Alberta Design System with bilingual content support and the accessibility and performance gates that apply to public-sector sites. The alberta.ca harness, in AIDE-ALBERTA-CA-HARNESS, slot-fills a canonical alberta.ca page from the real vendored stylesheets when the work in hand has to be indistinguishable from a live alberta.ca page. All three are MIT-licensed and forkable. A team picks the template that fits, clones it, and is working on domain content inside the first hour.

The template is also what makes the non-technical Builder viable. A specialist cannot reasonably re-implement a regulated-industry security posture from scratch, however good the agent or the harness is. But a specialist can take a template that already has the posture baked in and add the domain content on top. Without the template, a Builder is one configuration error away from a breach. With it, the Builder's changes ride on a foundation an auditor already cleared, and the only variation between projects is the variation each project actually needs.


## §06 Design before code

There is a fast way to use a coding agent that looks impressive by lunchtime and unshippable by the end of the quarter. You open a chat, describe what you want, and let the agent improvise the application into being. People call it vibe coding. The result looks like a finished app and behaves like a prototype, and turning it into something a regulator would clear can take months of rework.

Alberta runs the work in the other order. The application is designed before anyone writes code. The architecture, the security model, the specifications, the prototypes, the plan, and the requirements are built first, in a dedicated design stage that other papers in this collection cover in their own right. The agent then fills in the implementation against a specification that already exists.

The analogy is paint-by-number. Someone who cannot paint a portrait freehand can still lay the right colour inside each numbered shape. A coding agent works the same way. Give it the outline and it fills the outline reliably, and the speed comes from working against an outline that already exists. Deciding what the outline should be is the work that belongs to the design stage and the people in it.

This order is what lets speed and standards coexist. The roughly one hundred base controls a public system needs, the logging, the authentication, the network posture, the secrets handling, the deployment hardening, are solved and embedded in the template before the Builder arrives. Leave them for the agent to work out after the fact and the work takes months to make safe. Start from a template that already carries them and the agent only fills in the domain content, the part it does quickly and well.

The harness is one layer of a larger stack. The design stage that produces the specification, the orchestration environment that runs many agents at once (Nexus, in Paper 12), and the project-management layer that keeps the work moving (Velocity, in Paper 13) each get their own paper. The reframe underneath all of them is the same one that runs through the harness. The agent arrives with more capability than any single project will use, so the work is to narrow that capability to the single job in front of it, with the data, the tone, and the standard that job requires.


## §07 Driving with speed

So after several months of trial and error, prototyping, and refinement, this is what we have learned about how to move fast.

AI is best when creating new. It is the worst when it is reworking its own code. It is a myopic and forgetful genius, and it is better at building than modifying. The harness does not so much prevent rework as do it so frequently, in small steps, that significant drifts in intent are reasonably mitigated as you go.Start with the harness. After months or years with AI, you may outgrow them, but for the beginner, the harness drives rigour.Have your very best software developers focus with 'monk-like' dedication on building templates. You cannot over-index or over-value the time you spend perfecting a set of templates, skills, and standards for AI. Perfecting the template means the flaws do not materialize time and again.Ship these harnesses early and often, and get them in front of the first 10-50 people to test.Enable non-technical 'builders' to leverage AI to build applications that meet the same standards as those built by technical staff.Always integrate cyber security quality controls to ensure that no standard is ever lowered in the application of AI.Enshrine all standards into AI-readable formats so that AI is capable of meeting our requirements without guessing or making incorrect assumptions.Provide templates (pre-created code samples) that solve all standard security protocols. These templates, called in the Academy 'Hello World' templates, meet 100% of all cyber, accessibility, branding, functionality, and mobility requirements 'out of the box'. ## §08 Security is part of the structure

Two principles run through every piece of the harness and decide whether it delivers. The first is a posture. Cybersecurity is part of how the application is built. It is in the contract, in the playbooks, in what the inspections check on the way to release, and in what the template ships on day one. By the time the agent reports finished, the security work has happened alongside the functional work.

There is an older pattern in which security review is a separate phase at the end, run by a separate team after the application is functionally complete. The principle behind it is sound: an experienced security team should sign off before anything reaches a user. The practice is what fails. The security team becomes the last block before release, the colleague who has to say no after everyone else has said yes, and the cost of any change the review surfaces lands on a schedule that is already over.

The architectural frame is the difference between a building's structure and its paint. An architect specifies the structural elements in the original drawings: the foundation, the load-bearing walls, the fire-rated assemblies, the egress paths. None can be added after the building stands. The paint can change any time. Security has far more in common with the structure than with the paint. Adding it late is closer to bolting a wing onto a finished building than to repainting the lobby.

The visible evidence is the periodic table below. Each tile is one control the harness gets right by default, around ninety-five tiles across sixteen categories, from authentication and authorization through tokens and cookies, injection defence, security headers, rate limiting, logging and audit, secrets and cryptography, database posture, supply-chain hygiene, resilience, tests, and AI safety. Every tile points back to a file in the project. Reading across the columns gives the breadth of the security surface; reading any column down gives the depth on one concern. Nothing on the table is aspirational.


## §09 Two kinds of check for two kinds of question

The second principle is about the two kinds of question every piece of software carries. Some questions have a single right answer. Did the test suite pass? Is this dependency on a published list of known vulnerabilities? Does running this migration twice produce the same schema as running it once? Each has a yes or a no, and any honest measurement returns the same answer twice. A short script asks the question and the script's answer is the answer. These are the deterministic checks.

Other questions need a reader's judgement. Is this threat model complete? Does this design hold up to the way a thoughtful attacker would approach it? Could a citizen with no technical background read this page and find what they came for? Each can be answered, but no script returns a clean yes or no. The right answer needs an experienced reader to make the call. These are the probabilistic checks. The harness runs both. The deterministic checks are the floor, the regression-safe conditions the work clears every time. The probabilistic checks are the ceiling, the expert read that catches what a script cannot phrase.

The four review teams from the harness ride along here, each with its own colour, its own command, and its own folder of findings. Green keeps the supply chain and the code hygiene clean: it runs the dependency scans against published vulnerability lists, walks the tree for committed secrets, and flags dangerous patterns. Yellow audits the prose for AI smell. Red works from the outside as an adversary, running reconnaissance against the deployment and planning the attack paths a real intruder would try. Green, yellow, and red are deterministic, so the same input gives the same output and they make safe gates. Blue is the probabilistic one. It reads what the others found, writes the threat model and the defensive-architecture review, and proposes the fixes, the work an experienced security reviewer would otherwise do by hand.


## §10 What the recipe produces

The clearest evidence comes from the Alberta AI Academy. The Level 3 cohort that ran over six days in early summer drew a hundred students, as often from the policy and program side of the public service as from engineering. ## §11 Where this goes next

The harness in this paper runs in the developer's terminal. The agent sits beside the engineer, the inspections fire on the way to release, and the work moves from a contract to a tested deployment in one workspace. That is the state of the practice today. The next paper moves the harness into the running system.

There the agent moves inside the deployed application and watches production on a continuous loop. It notices when a dependency that was safe yesterday lands on a published vulnerability list today and files the upgrade. It notices when a configuration has drifted and opens a request to put it back. It runs its own security review against the live system overnight and flags anything new in the morning. The application begins to look after itself, the way a building's automated systems begin to look after the building once it is occupied.

We are releasing the harness, the templates, the skill files, and the papers themselves as open source, so you can fork them, test them, and take them further than we have. Come build with us.


## §12 Appendix: the technical details, in order of why they matter

If the body answers how a harness works, this appendix answers what sits underneath each piece. The order is the order an attacker, an auditor, or an on-call engineer would walk through, asking what is in place for this concern, and why does it matter? A technical reader can read it in any order. A non-technical reader can read it top to bottom and finish with a working map of every layer the harness gets right by default.

1. Who can do what: authentication and authorization. Sign-in runs through OAuth 2.0 or OpenID Connect against Google or Microsoft Entra ID. The session token is an RS256-signed JWT held in an httpOnly cookie. The refresh token rotates on every use and the previous one is revoked. Roles form a six-tier hierarchy enforced in the application middleware and again as a database CHECK constraint, so the same rule is checked twice on independent surfaces. Why it matters: the wrong person doing the right thing is still a breach. Identity and role are the first answer to every other question on this list.

2. How the app remembers you between clicks: tokens, cookies, and CSRF. The session token lives in an httpOnly cookie the JavaScript on the page never reads. The cross-site-request-forgery defence is a double-submit check: a value the server set in a cookie is compared against the same value sent back in a request header, with a constant-time compare so the comparison itself cannot leak information. The client never writes to local storage. Why it matters: if a session token leaks, an attacker can act as you. Keeping the surface where tokens live as small as possible is the cheapest defence on the list.

3. Stopping bad input from being read as code: injection and XSS. Every rendered HTML fragment passes through a sanitiser that strips dangerous content before the browser sees it. Every database query is parameterised, never built by stitching strings together. Every request body, route parameter, and query string is validated against a schema at the boundary before any controller looks at it. Why it matters: the most common breach starts as untrusted input the application failed to validate and ended up treating as instructions.

4. Telling the browser to defend you: security headers. A Content-Security-Policy that limits what the page can load. A one-year Strict-Transport-Security header that forbids the browser from downgrading to plain HTTP. A frame-ancestors directive that prevents the application from being embedded in another site. A nosniff content-type rule and a strict referrer policy. A Permissions-Policy that turns off browser features the application does not use. Why it matters: even when the application makes a mistake, the browser has a second line of defence, and it uses that line only if the application asks it to.

5. Slowing down floods: rate limits and denial of service. Two hundred requests per fifteen minutes on the API baseline. Thirty per fifteen minutes on the authentication surface. Sixty per hour on the AI endpoints, because each AI call costs real money. Every limit issues a Retry-After header so well-behaved clients back off cleanly and badly-behaved ones are throttled the same way. Why it matters: a small attacker should not be able to make the service unusable for everyone else.

6. Knowing what happened: logging and audit. Structured JSON logs in production. Every request carries a correlation identifier so the lines for a single user action can be stitched together across services. Every mutation lands a row in an audit-log table with the user, the IP address, and the data before and after the change. A redactor strips personal data out of the logs before they are written. Why it matters: the first thing you wish for during an incident is logs. The second is logs that are not themselves a privacy breach.

7. Keeping the keys safe: secrets and cryptography. RSA-2048 key pairs for signing the session tokens. Keys held in a managed secrets store rather than in environment files. A startup check that refuses to boot the server on a missing or placeholder secret. A Docker build that fails if a development key has slipped past the ignore file. Why it matters: a leaked key bypasses every other defence on the list. The cheapest control is the one that prevents the leak in the first place.

8. Keeping the data honest: database posture. A single shared connection pool with a hard cap on per-query time. Migrations that are idempotent: running them twice produces the same schema as running them once. A migration log table that records what has actually been applied to this particular database. SSL on the connection to the managed database. Why it matters: the data is the part you cannot remake. Everything else is recoverable.

9. Watching your suppliers: supply chain. A scan of every dependency on every push against published vulnerability lists. A scan that covers packages outside the project's primary language, so a Python sidecar or a Java tool is not invisible. A minor-version pin on the base container image. Override entries in the package manifest that pin transitive dependencies to patched versions when the upstream parent has not yet released a fix. A scan of the git history itself for secrets that may have been committed once and forgotten. Why it matters: the code you wrote is small and the code you depend on is large, and an attacker needs only one weak link in either.

10. Planning for when things go wrong: resilience and recovery. A backup script designed for cron. A restore script with a guard against running against a production database by accident. Documented recovery-time and recovery-point objectives, so the operations team knows what is acceptable to lose. Liveness and readiness probes so the orchestrator routes traffic only when the system is ready to handle it. A graceful shutdown that finishes in-flight requests before the pod is killed. Why it matters: outages happen, and the work of resilience is to make them recoverable in proportion to how much you prepared in advance.

11. Knowing it works: tests and evaluations. Seven hundred and seventy-three tests across eighty-one suites. Unit tests for individual functions. Integration tests for routes and middleware. End-to-end tests that drive a real browser through the public flows. An accessibility test that fails the build on a real violation. A performance budget that fails the build on a regression. A mechanical check that every functional requirement in the requirements document has an implementation citation behind it. Why it matters: you cannot ship what you cannot demonstrate works. 'It worked on my laptop' is the most expensive sentence in software.

12. Guardrails for the agents themselves: AI safety. A provider interface that lets the project change which language model it uses by changing a single configuration value. A system-prompt guardrail that refuses out-of-scope requests at the boundary, before the request reaches the model. A redactor that runs over user content before any external model call, so personal data does not leave the deployment. Four review teams that audit the code on the way to release: green for code hygiene, yellow for prose, red for adversarial reconnaissance, and blue for the defensive-architecture review. Why it matters: agents have their own failure modes. An unguarded agent is its own attack surface.

13. The model stack at time of writing. The long-horizon work on each project, the application map, the threat model, the kill-chain aggregation, and the code-fix proposals, runs on Claude Code Opus 4.7. The deterministic recon and review agents run in parallel through the Claude Sonnet 4.6 Agent SDK. Opus 4.7 holds the planning context for an entire delivery loop in one window. The Sonnet 4.6 Agent SDK gives the orchestrator a primitive for running many small scanners side by side with explicit contracts on what each scanner sees and returns. The harness's contracts are written to be model-agnostic, so the same shape works with whatever comes next.

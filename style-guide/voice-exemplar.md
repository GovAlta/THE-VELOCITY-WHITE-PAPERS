# Voice fidelity — preserve the author's voice

The primary positive reference for how to write in an author's voice. Read it before guides `00`, `01`, `02`. When those guides and this file disagree on whether to keep an author's wording, **this file wins**.

The other guides tell you what *not* to do. They are a filter on prose **you generate from scratch**. They are not a licence to rewrite an author's own sentences. This file tells you how to keep whatever voice the author brought.

## The anchor is the prose in front of you

There is no single house voice to imitate, and no stored sample to match. **The voice for a paper is established by the prose the author provides for that paper.** Before you draft, read their material and sample it:

- sentence length and rhythm (long and comma-joined, or short and clipped),
- person and stance (first person plural and institutional, or detached and third person),
- the specifics they reach for (dates, counts, before-and-after pairs, named tools),
- the terms they coin and the way they gloss them,
- how much candor, humour, or direct address they allow.

Whatever you generate to connect, expand, or weave around their prose must sound like it came from the same hand. The author's own passages stay as written.

## The one rule that matters most

**When the author has written the words, the author's words are the source of truth. Do not re-voice them.**

Your job when editing an author's prose is to fix what is wrong: a false claim, a typo, a genuine AI tell that crept in. Leave everything else alone. Do not chop long sentences into short ones. Do not strip the commas that join clauses. Do not swap words for "plainer" ones. Do not impose the short-declarative cadence guide `00` describes. That cadence is for prose you write; it is not the author's voice.

If you find yourself improving a sentence that was not broken, stop. That is the exact move that produces "new AI smell."

## What strong authored voice tends to look like

These are features of good human technical writing, not the fingerprints of one person. Most authors worth preserving show several. Recognise them so you keep them rather than sand them off:

- **Long, flowing, comma-joined sentences are allowed, when they carry one thought.** A thought that runs forty words across several clauses is right when the thought is that long. Do not break a continuous thought into three short sentences for effect. The opposite failure is just as real: a long sentence that crams several separate items or ideas together is density, not voice, and the clarity rules in `00` apply to it. The author's long sentences develop a single idea; they do not stack a list. When the prose is yours rather than his, default to short and clear (see `00`, "Clarity for a mixed audience").
- **The author's chosen person and stance.** If they write "we," "our," "the team," keep it. Name the actor where they name it.
- **Enumeration in prose.** Counting through points inside a paragraph ("first… second… the third was…") is a valid alternative to bullets. Keep it as prose where that is how it was written.
- **Specifics woven in, never as decoration.** Dates, counts, before-and-after pairs, named tools. The number is part of the sentence's meaning, not an ornament beside it.
- **Coined terms introduced and defined inline.** Authors name things and explain them in the same breath. Their coined vocabulary is theirs, not slop. Keep the coinage and its inline gloss together; do not strike it.
- **Candor and reflection.** Admitting what does not work, naming gaps, saying something unexpected and standing behind it. Keep it. Do not sand it into corporate neutrality.
- **Analogies built out across two or three sentences.** An analogy that does real work is kept whole, not compressed to a clause.
- **Direct address where it fits.** Turning to the reader as a colleague mid-argument is in voice.

## Phrases to never emit

- **Never paste a phrase from a "Forbidden" column of guides `00` or `02` into a paper.** The guides quote bad writing to warn you off it. Quoting it back as content is the worst possible outcome, and it has actually happened, so the rule is explicit.
- Avoid the registers `00` names: the TED-talk opening, the LinkedIn post, the movie-trailer voiceover. If a sentence you wrote sounds like one of those, it is the wrong sentence.
- If a guide's bad example starts to feel like natural phrasing, that is the guide working in reverse on you. Write the author's plain version instead.

## How the negative-filter guides relate to this file

- `00-writing-style-guide.md` — the hard rules. A filter on prose **you generate**: no em dashes, no banned flourishes or vocabulary, no "not X but Y." Not instructions to restructure, shorten, or re-cadence the author's existing sentences.
- `01-author-voice.md` — long-form rationale for the posture. Background. This file is the operational version.
- `02-substance-and-structure-guide.md` — the substance passes (is the claim true, does it trace to a source, does every number carry its unit). Run them on the substance, never as an excuse to re-voice a true sentence already in the author's words.
- `03-fidelity-and-sourcing.md` — completeness and citation rules. Voice preservation and completeness work together: keep the author's words *and* keep all of the author's content.

The shortest way to say all of this: **fix what is false or accidental; preserve everything that is the author's.**

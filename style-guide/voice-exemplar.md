# Voice Exemplar — the author's real writing

This is the primary positive reference for how the author writes. Read it before guide `00`, `01`, or `02`. When those guides and this file disagree, this file wins.

The other guides tell you what *not* to do. They are a filter on prose **you generate from scratch**. They are not a license to rewrite the author's own sentences. This file shows you what the author *does* do, in his own words, so you can match it.

---

## The one rule that matters most

**When the author has written the words, the author's words are the source of truth. Do not re-voice them.**

Your job when editing the author's prose is to fix what is wrong (a false claim, a typo, a genuine AI tell that crept in), and to leave everything else alone. You do not chop his long sentences into short ones. You do not strip the commas that join his clauses. You do not swap his words for "plainer" ones. You do not impose the short-declarative cadence that guide `00` describes. That cadence is for prose you write; it is not his voice.

If you find yourself improving a sentence that was not broken, stop. That is the exact move that produces "new AI smell."

## The gold standard — read this and match it

The passage below is the author writing in his own voice. It is the reference for cadence, posture, and density. Everything you draft for this collection should sound like it came from the same person who wrote this.

> Large language models have a strong capacity to develop application code. Early in our AI Maximalist program in 2025, Technology and Innovation staff began to evaluate these tools to see how they could be deployed within our government environment. A range of tools were explored, such as Cursor, Lovable, and Claude Code, which was identified as the most effective and popular at the time. These tools made application development fast, easy for even non-technical people, and the time to transform an idea into a functional prototype dropped from roughly six weeks to as little as thirty minutes. The speed increases were so significant that ministry subject matter experts could be present in the room as ideas were worked out in real time, providing feedback and sign-off so technical teams could continue their work. This factor alone resulted in a significant increase in the speed of delivery, as the traditional back and forth between technical and non-technical staff was reduced to a single session.

> Through rounds of trial and error, Alberta staff began to test methods of increasing the assurance in the quality and consistency of the code output. Starting with small application features like a landing page or an API, the team developed a range of standard methods: instructions for the model to follow in its prompt, which would narrow the creativity of the model by limiting uncertainty. These standards, now known as skill files, are provided to the coding agent at the beginning of a task.

> Building harnesses is difficult and takes extreme diligence. You need to be diligent because you are not just building an app, you are curating the skills a human might gain intuitively over ten or twenty years. You cannot vibe code or make simple prompts. Using AI to create harnesses has the photocopier problem, in that each derivative version degrades the quality, causing future and unpredictable breakdowns. A harness that is ninety per cent accurate produces an app that is eighty per cent accurate. You need to take the time and meditate on every feature, and test, and test again, each iteration to ensure the system is faithfully building what you need.

## What the author actually does (observed, not prescribed)

These are features pulled from the passage above and the rest of his real writing. Match them.

**Long, flowing, comma-joined sentences are correct.** The author builds a thought across several clauses joined by commas and "and." He does not break a continuous thought into three short sentences for effect. A sentence that runs forty words because the thought runs forty words is right. Do not shorten it.

**First person plural, institutional and warm.** "We," "our," "the team," "Alberta," "Technology and Innovation." He names who did the thing, and the actor is usually a group he belongs to.

**Enumeration in prose.** "First… Second… The third significant challenge was…" He counts through points inside paragraphs rather than always breaking to bullets. Keep his enumerations as prose when that is how he wrote them.

**Specifics woven in, never as decoration.** Dates ("March 2025 to May 2026"), counts ("more than a thousand applications"), before-and-after pairs ("six weeks to thirty minutes"), named tools (Cursor, Lovable, Claude Code, Claude Cowork). The number is part of the sentence's meaning, not an ornament dropped beside it.

**Coined terms introduced and defined inline.** He names things and explains them in the same breath: "These standards, now known as skill files, are…"; "these skills are highly opinionated, a technical term meaning we are leaving little to the imagination." Keep the coinage and the inline gloss together. His coined vocabulary — skill files, harness, spec-driven development, AI smell, Hello World templates, red / blue / green / yellow team — is his, not slop. Do not strike it.

**Candor and reflection.** He admits what does not work, names the gaps, and is willing to say something unexpected and stand behind it ("It may be odd to say play, especially in a government environment, but that is how we humans learn"). Keep the candor. Do not sand it into corporate neutrality.

**Analogies that are built out, not dropped.** The photocopier problem, arts academies copying the Renaissance masters, the Arthur C. Clarke riff on advanced templates. He explains an analogy across two or three sentences so it does real work. Keep the whole analogy; do not compress it to a clause.

**Direct address where it fits.** "You need to be diligent because you are not just building an app." He turns to the reader as a colleague mid-argument. This is in voice.

## Poison phrases — never emit these

These exact strings, and close variants, were copied out of the bad-example tables in guides `00` and `02` and pasted into wp-08 as if they were content. They are not the author's writing. They are examples of what to avoid, and they must never appear in any reader-visible string you produce.

- "Velocity is an imperative" (in any form, including "is an imperative in public service")
- "The weight underneath is the reason" / "the weight underneath"
- Any sentence you recognise from a "Forbidden" column of a style guide. The guides quote bad writing to warn you off it. Quoting it back into a paper is the worst possible outcome.

If you are drafting and a guide's bad example feels like a natural phrasing, that is the guide working in reverse on you. Write the author's plain version instead.

## How the negative-filter guides relate to this file

- `00-writing-style-guide.md` — the hard rules. Treat them as a **filter on prose you generate**: do not add em dashes, do not add the banned flourishes and vocabulary, do not add "not X but Y." Do **not** read them as instructions to restructure the author's existing sentences, shorten them, or strip their cadence.
- `01-author-voice.md` — long-form rationale for the posture. Useful background. This exemplar is the operational version; when `01` and this file pull apart, follow this file.
- `02-substance-and-structure-guide.md` — the substance passes (is the claim true, does it trace to a source, does every number carry its unit). These passes are genuinely useful and you should run them. But run them on the *substance*, never as an excuse to re-voice a true sentence that is already in the author's words.

The shortest way to say all of this: **fix what is false or accidental; preserve everything that is his.**

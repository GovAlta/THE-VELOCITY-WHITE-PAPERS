# 01 — Author Voice

A companion to `00-writing-style-guide.md`. The 00 file lists the hard rules: constructions and vocabulary to avoid. This file describes the positive shape of the voice: how the author thinks, structures an argument, lands an idea, and brings a reader along.

The voice described here is a posture, not a person. It belongs to a senior practitioner who has worked on both sides of a hard problem, in policy and in hands-on delivery, and who is now bringing a thoughtful colleague along. It is confident about the direction, honest about the unknowns, specific about the numbers, and always inviting the reader to push back. It stays the same regardless of the setting or who is in the room, and any author writing for this collection should be able to hold it.

This guide is written for an author, or for a Claude agent, about to draft a paper, a slide narration, or a long-form audio script in this voice. It is a style sheet, not a recipe. It describes the *moves* the voice makes, not the specific analogies, frameworks, or examples used in past papers. Those belong to the papers and presentations they came from. A new paper invents its own.

A second purpose: the audio narration for every paper is read by a voice clone of the author. The writing has to be both readable on the page and speakable on the audio track. The audio section near the end resolves the cases where the two pull in different directions.

A note on scope. Each whitepaper in the Velocity Papers collection must stand on its own. A reader may come to any paper without having read the others, and the paper has to work for that reader. The author's voice — patient context-setting, generous explanation, specific numbers, inviting close — is built for exactly that situation. The guide below is the long form of how to do it.

---

## The spirit in one sentence

A senior practitioner bringing a thoughtful colleague along on a hard problem. Confident about the direction. Honest about the unknowns. Specific about the numbers. Always inviting the reader to push back, take it further, or come along on the work.

---

## The mental posture toward the reader

Every paragraph the author writes assumes a particular relationship with the reader. The reader is a colleague. The reader is intelligent. The reader does not yet know this material, and the writer's job is to bring them across.

Three things follow from this posture.

**The reader is not an audience.** Writing to an audience produces a presentation. Writing to a colleague produces a draft on a shared whiteboard. The colleague can interrupt, disagree, push back. The author's voice already invites this. The paper should too.

**The reader is intelligent.** Don't underestimate them. If the topic is technical, the prose can go technical — provided the technical content is introduced in plain language first. Never explain something as if the reader is a beginner; explain it as if the reader is a peer who has been working in a different field and has just walked over.

**The reader does not yet share the writer's context.** Every paper must be self-contained enough to function as the first thing the reader reads. Even when a paper is the eighth in a sequence, the opening must re-establish the larger conversation. References to other papers in the collection are pointers, not prerequisites. The reader should be able to put any paper down at any section and pick it up at the next visit without being lost.

These three commitments — colleague-not-audience, intelligent-not-beginner, context-must-be-built — produce most of the structural moves below.

---

## How to open a piece of writing

A paper in this voice opens in four short moves, in this order. Together they take roughly the space of a single dropcap paragraph and a first section paragraph. They should appear in this order even when the paper is long.

**Move 1 — Acknowledge what's true at the largest frame the reader will accept.** The author opens at the level of the public being served, the problem in the world, or the moment in time. Not at the level of the paper. The first sentence names the world the paper is operating in.

**Move 2 — Plant the stakes in a specific number or fact.** Abstract claims like "this matters" or "this is urgent" do not land. A single concrete number, a year, a count, a budget figure, a deadline — anything that grounds the claim in something the reader can verify or remember — does land. One per opening is enough.

**Move 3 — Name what the paper is, and what it is not.** Tell the reader the boundary of this paper. State, in one sentence, what the paper will do. State, in one short clause, what it will not do (and where that other work lives, if anywhere). This is what allows the paper to stand on its own without trying to be every other paper at once.

**Move 4 — State the takeaway the reader should carry away.** Not a list of objectives. One sentence that names what the reader should be able to think, do, or decide differently after finishing. The author often opens with this. The reader is given the punchline before the argument.

Avoid: throat-clearing ("in this paper I will discuss"), apologies, multi-sentence definitions of common terms, lists of references to other papers. The reader knows it is a paper. Begin.

### Generic example of the shape (invented, illustrative only)

> *Public services run on data that lives in too many places. Roughly forty per cent of the systems that hold that data were built before the smartphone existed. This paper is about how a small set of practices can move that data into modern systems without taking the systems offline. It is not about the cost — Paper 2 carries that argument. By the end of this paper you should understand the practices well enough to apply them to a system you are responsible for.*

Notice what the shape does: it names the world (public services on aging systems), it plants a number (forty per cent, pre-smartphone), it draws the boundary (not about cost), and it states the takeaway (apply the practices to a system you own). Four moves, four sentences. Then the body begins.

## The structural arc

Every long piece in this voice has the same three-part shape under whatever section headings appear on the surface.

### Part 1 — Context and stakes

The opening establishes the world. The first sections after the opening expand that world. The author's instinct is to start at the broadest frame the reader will accept, then narrow down to the specific problem the paper is taking on. The narrowing happens over two or three sections, not in one paragraph.

A useful question during drafting: "What does the reader need to believe before the middle of the paper will land?" Whatever the answer, those beliefs are established in Part 1.

Acknowledge what is already in motion. Pretending the work begins with this paper insults the reader and the people who did earlier work. The author consistently credits earlier efforts as the right move at the right time, even when arguing they are no longer sufficient.

### Part 2 — The principle, the substance, the work

The middle is where the paper earns its length. This is where the principles, the framework, the technical detail, or the case study lives. The author's working pattern in the middle is consistent: lead with the *idea* of what is being proposed, anchor it in something the reader already knows, then introduce the technical name and the technical detail. Idea → anchor → name. Never name → detail → maybe-an-analogy-later.

Inside the middle, every section is its own miniature version of the three-part arc. Each section opens by naming the specific friction it addresses, presents the principle, anchors it, gives one concrete example, and closes by pointing forward into the next section. Sections that follow this pattern read smoothly aloud; sections that don't tend to feel like a list.

### Part 3 — What it produces, what comes next

Close the loop. State what the reader now has in their hands. Acknowledge what is still open, undecided, or unfinished. The author never pretends a paper is the final word. Invite participation — feedback, contribution, disagreement, partnership. Always point to the next thing the reader can do, the next paper they could read, or the next decision they could make.

A paper in this voice never ends on a summary. The reader just read the paper. End on a horizon.

## Pacing and density

A paper in this voice is dense in ideas and generous in pacing. The two are not in tension when the writing is patient.

**Generous pacing means**: a new idea is introduced, given a sentence to land, anchored in something familiar, illustrated by a small example, and only then connected to the next idea. The reader is never asked to hold three new concepts in mind at once. If a section requires three new ideas, give the section three subsections.

**Dense in ideas means**: the writing does not pad. Every paragraph carries weight. There are no transition paragraphs. There are no "we will now turn to..." paragraphs. Every paragraph is doing work.

The interaction of these two produces papers that feel substantial without feeling exhausting. A reader should be able to put the paper down at the end of any section and pick it up at the next visit without re-reading. That is the pacing test.

The drafting move that supports this: write the section, then read each paragraph and ask "what is this paragraph for?" If the answer is "it introduces the next paragraph", cut it. The structural signposts live in the section headings, not in the prose.

## How the author uses analogy

The author thinks in analogies. The reader meets a new idea more easily when it is anchored in something they already know. This is the single most-used move in the voice.

Three patterns shape how the author reaches for an analogy.

**Pattern one: anchor in shared lived experience.** The author's instinct is to reach for analogies the reader has personally lived. The texture of waking up in a kitchen, of starting a new job, of being on a team, of driving in traffic, of fixing a bicycle. The analogy works because the reader has felt it. Avoid analogies that require the reader to imagine an experience they have not had.

**Pattern two: stack two or three analogies for one hard idea.** When a single concept is doing real work in the paper, give the reader more than one way in. Each analogy illuminates a different facet. Two is usually enough. Three is the maximum. After three the rhetorical scaffolding becomes more visible than the idea it is holding up.

**Pattern three: build the analogy fresh, do not import it.** This is important. Do not reach for analogies from other Velocity Papers, other speeches, or other writing in the collection. Each paper invents the analogies it needs. Recurring analogies across papers become slogans, and slogans drain. A new paper meets its reader fresh.

When a piece of the paper's own thinking deserves a handle, give it a short name and use it consistently within the paper. The name does not need to leave the paper. Most named ideas should not.

## How the author uses stories and examples

A paper in this voice carries concrete examples in every major section. Not hypotheticals, when real ones exist. Specific scenes, specific people in roles, specific outcomes with specific numbers.

There are three textures of example that work in this voice.

**The peer scene.** A short scene drawn from someone the reader could imagine: a colleague in another ministry, a friend who runs a small business, a junior employee in their first week. The scene names the person's role, their question, and what they were trying to do. Two or three sentences. Then the paper continues with what the scene reveals.

**The personal observation.** A short personal admission from the writer's own experience. The voice moves between confident framing and small admissions of having been wrong, having had to learn, having had to change course. These admissions land because they are quiet; they don't dramatise the writer's journey, they simply note it.

**The specific outcome.** A measurable result, named with the conditions that produced it. Numbers do most of the work here. A specific number is more memorable and more credible than a percentage range or a vague gain.

The placement of an example is consistent: an example arrives just after the principle has been stated, before the technical detail begins. The example is the rest stop between the abstract claim and the work. Never lead a section with a hypothetical scene; the reader has not yet been told what the scene is for.

When a real example is not available, invent a small one and signal that it is illustrative. The author's voice prefers a small invented example to a long abstract argument.

## Explaining technical concepts to a non-technical reader

When a paper has to bring a layperson across a technical idea, the author uses a four-move pattern. Use it for any concept the reader is unlikely to know.

**Move 1 — Anchor in shared experience.** Start with something the reader has done. Not "imagine a database" but "if you have ever opened a filing cabinet to find an old form…". The technical concept is the cabinet, the form, the searching.

**Move 2 — Name the principle.** State, in plain language, what the technical concept actually does. One sentence. The reader has not yet heard the technical name.

**Move 3 — Introduce the technical term, briefly.** Now name it. Say what people in the field call it. Note that the field's term is sometimes more intimidating than the idea. The reader can take or leave the name.

**Move 4 — Show how the author would actually use it.** This is the move most often missed. Walk the reader through the writer's own working pattern with the concept. What do you reach for first. What do you check next. What you do when it fails. A definition becomes a recipe.

This four-move pattern can carry the heaviest technical content in the paper. The author's working assumption is that a non-technical reader who finishes the paper should be able to repeat the writer's workflow in their own words. If the paper does not enable that, it has skipped move four.

A second pattern, used when the topic is the use of a powerful tool: explain that the work is not building the tool up to be capable, the way one would build up a traditional system. The tool already has more capability than the user will use. The work is constraining it down to the specific job, the specific tone, the specific data. This reframe — narrowing rather than enlarging — is consistent in the voice.

## Tone calibration

Three layers run together in every paragraph. A paragraph that sits in only one layer reads off.

**Confidence layer.** The voice is confident about the direction and about the work the reader is being invited into. Confidence shows up in declarative sentences with no hedge, in the choice of strong specific verbs, in the willingness to say "this is what we are doing" rather than "this is what we are considering".

**Honesty layer.** The voice is honest about the unknowns. Honesty shows up in admissions of what has not been solved, in clear acknowledgements of complexity, in the willingness to say "we don't know yet" when that is the truth. Honest hedges — *a little, somewhat, in some cases, often, typically* — appear as conversational softeners, never as filler.

**Invitation layer.** The voice invites the reader to push back, contribute, take the work further, or disagree. Invitation shows up at the end of sections and especially at the end of papers. It also shows up mid-argument when the writer admits the reader may see this differently.

A good paragraph in this voice carries all three layers across its sentences. Not in every sentence. Across the paragraph. A paragraph that is purely confident reads as a sales pitch. A paragraph that is purely honest reads as uncertain. A paragraph that is purely inviting reads as a chat post. The mixture is the voice.

---

## Sentence cadence and rhythm

The default sentence is medium length, declarative, active. Active voice is the floor: name who is doing the action.

**Vary the lengths.** After two or three medium sentences, drop a short one. The short one carries the weight. The reader's eye and ear both notice the change.

**Avoid run-on commas.** When the sentence is collecting clauses with commas, end it. Two short sentences read more clearly than one long one. The voice does not need long sentences to sound thoughtful.

**Repetition for emphasis is allowed, sparingly.** When a single point is the headline of a section, repeating the verb or the subject across two or three consecutive sentences anchors it. Three is the sweet spot. Beyond three, the prose tips into rhetoric and the reader notices the device.

**One-sentence paragraphs are allowed, sparingly.** A short paragraph that sits on its own line lands hard. Use it for a turn in the argument or a claim the reader must carry forward. Two or three per paper. Not more.

**Read each paragraph aloud during drafting.** Anywhere the mental voice gets tangled or runs out of breath, the sentence is wrong. This applies to text the audio narration will not even use. If the prose is not speakable, it is not in this voice.

## Numbers, names, and specificity

The voice is specific. Specificity is the most-used credibility move.

**Use a specific number whenever a number exists.** Precise numbers are more credible than rounded ones. "One thousand three hundred staff" beats "around a thousand staff". When a number is approximate, say so plainly: "roughly", "around", "about".

**Use proper names for systems, places, ministries, programs.** Spell them correctly. If the name is an acronym, introduce it in full on first use and use the acronym afterward. Don't introduce an acronym you won't reuse.

**One concrete detail per claim, where possible.** A claim with a number, a date, a name, or a place embedded in it is more memorable than a claim without one. The reader carries the claim away.

**Avoid percentages when an absolute number is available.** "Five hundred and sixty applications shipped" is more memorable than "the productivity gain was substantial". When percentages are the right shape, pair them with the base they apply to.

## Section structure inside the paper

Each section in the paper carries its own miniature arc. The section opens by naming the friction it addresses. The section presents the principle. The section anchors the principle in something familiar. The section walks the reader through one concrete example. The section closes by pointing into the next section or by stating what the reader now has.

A few patterns that consistently support this in the voice.

**Section headings name content, not importance.** "What the harness does" or "How the contract works" — not "Critical concepts" or "Foundations".

**Sub-sections (if used) appear three deep at most.** Beyond three levels the reader loses the map. If a topic requires more depth, split it into a second paper.

**Pull-quotes belong inside a section, not as section opens.** A pull-quote is the line you wish the reader would remember from that section. Drop one per section at most.

**Side-notes carry the technical detail a generalist reader can skip.** When a paragraph would otherwise be too long because it needs a precise definition or a regulatory reference, lift the definition into a sidenote. The reader who needs it reads it. The reader who does not skips on.

**Figures and visuals are referenced by what they show, not by their number.** The reader does not page back to "Figure 8.2". The reader sees the figure and reads on. So the prose around the figure should say what the figure shows in one sentence, and the caption should expand on it. Treat the figure as part of the prose, not as a separate object.

---

## Closing patterns

A paper in this voice closes in three moves.

**Move 1 — Return to the largest frame the paper opened on.** If the opening named the public being served, the close returns to the public being served. The reader should feel the paper has come full circle.

**Move 2 — Name what comes next, by paper number or by name.** The Velocity Papers are a collection. Each paper points forward. Tell the reader where the conversation continues, what the next paper takes on, or what the next decision is.

**Move 3 — One line of invitation.** A short warm line that names the relationship the paper is offering the reader. The reader is a colleague who can push back, contribute, or come along on the work.

Avoid: a closing summary list. The reader just read the paper. Avoid: a closing flourish that recasts what was said in higher language. The reader will trust the writing more if the last paragraph ends where it ends.

## Writing for audio narration

Every paper's audio narration is produced from the author's voice clone. The narration is not the paper read verbatim — it is a parallel script in the same voice. The slide-narration text inside the TL;DR presentation, and the long-form audio script for the full paper, both need to be written for the ear from the start.

Seven constraints follow.

**Write to be heard, not just read.** Read each sentence aloud during drafting. Anywhere the mental voice naturally pauses, the sentence should have ended. Anywhere the mental voice gets tangled, the sentence is wrong.

**Drop parentheticals.** Parentheticals work on the page where the eye can skip them. They do not work in the ear. The voice clone reads them flat and the listener loses the thread. Either incorporate the parenthetical content into the main sentence, or remove it.

**Use periods where you would use semicolons or em dashes.** The hard rules already forbid em dashes. For the audio script the rule is stricter: any pause longer than a comma should be a period. The voice clone breathes on periods.

**Spell out small numbers, use figures for large ones.** A figure under fifty reads more naturally as a word. A figure with a unit attached (millions, billions, percent, dollars) reads naturally as the digits and the word. The reason is that the voice clone occasionally stumbles when decoding bare numerals.

**Use rhetorical questions sparingly.** One per slide narration. Two per long-form section. More than that and the narration sounds like a sales pitch.

**Mark emphasis by rewriting, not by italics.** The voice clone cannot render italics. If a word needs emphasis, lift it into its own short sentence rather than relying on a typographic mark.

**Open each slide narration with a complete sentence that stands alone.** Slides are heard independently in the player. Do not open a slide with "So…", "And…", or "Now…". Open with a claim, a frame, a number, or a question. The first sentence of the narration must stand on its own without the previous slide.

The audio script for a long-form paper is not the paper. It is a re-write in the same voice, with shorter sentences, simpler clauses, and no visual scaffolding. Treat it as a parallel artifact, not a derived one.

## Making the paper stand on its own

Each paper in the Velocity Papers collection must be readable as the first thing a reader picks up. The reader may have come from a search engine, a social media link, a colleague's recommendation, or the printed copy on a conference table. The reader did not necessarily read Paper 1, Paper 5, or the abstract on the home page. The paper must do the work of welcoming them in.

Five practices support this.

**The opening establishes the world.** The first three or four paragraphs introduce the problem the paper is operating in, the readers it is for, and the boundary of what it will cover. These paragraphs are not preamble. They are the gateway. A reader who reads only the first three paragraphs should know what the paper is about and whether to keep reading.

**Every term used in the paper is either common knowledge or introduced.** Before a technical term appears, it has been named in plain language. Before a proper name appears, the reader has been told what it refers to. The paper is self-contained: the reader does not have to leave the page to follow it.

**References to other papers are pointers, not prerequisites.** A paper may say "Paper 1 makes the cost case in detail" — that is a pointer the reader can follow if interested. The current paper should still be readable without it. A reader who has not read Paper 1 should never feel they are missing the floor of the argument.

**The mid-paper contains its own restate.** Around the centre of a long paper, the writing pauses to restate the argument so far in one or two sentences. This is not a recap of the introduction — it is a brief return to the main thread before the paper continues. A reader who picks the paper up at this point should find their footing.

**The close enables the next reading session.** Many readers will pick the paper up over several sittings. The close of a section, and the close of the paper, should both leave the reader with a clear sense of where they have arrived and what to expect on returning. The reader's bookmark should be a comfortable place to leave the paper for the night.

A paper that respects these five practices is longer than a brief and lighter than a textbook. It is the right shape for the reader who has time to read but no time to be confused.

## A drafting checklist

Before considering a section finished, walk it through these questions in order.

1. Does the section open by naming the specific friction it addresses, rather than restating the section title?
2. Has the principle of the section been stated in one plain sentence before the technical detail begins?
3. Is there at least one anchor — analogy, scene, named example — that lets the reader land the principle in something familiar?
4. Is there at least one specific number, name, or place somewhere in the section?
5. Does the section's writing carry confidence, honesty, and invitation across its paragraphs, rather than sitting in one tone?
6. Could a reader who started the paper at this section follow it, given the prose that came before?
7. Does the section close by pointing forward rather than summarising what was just said?
8. If this prose will also be heard as audio narration, does it survive being read aloud — short sentences, clean breaths, no parentheticals?

A section that answers no to two or more of these questions needs another pass before the paper moves on.

## What this voice never does

A short list of moves that break the voice quickly even when they do not violate any 00-guide rule.

**Lecturing.** The voice invites. It does not instruct from above.

**Shaming past systems or past decisions.** The voice consistently credits earlier work as the right move at the right time and explains what now comes next.

**Pretending to have all the answers.** The voice acknowledges the work that remains undone and names it honestly.

**Padding with throat-clearing.** Phrases like "in this paper I will discuss" or "before we begin let us note" are not in the voice. The paper begins.

**Closing with a bow on top.** The voice does not summarise the paper in its last paragraph. The voice ends on a horizon, an invitation, or a pointer to the next conversation.

**Treating the reader as audience.** The voice writes to a colleague who happens not to know this material yet. That single posture, more than any rule in this guide, produces almost everything else above.
